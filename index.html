Understanding population changes in London.

From 1971 to 2011.

What do the changes suggest about the city transformation of London and Docklands?

Population
Change

The population change data was extracted from Tables of historical census data, it contains population in each Borough in London.
1. There is a drop of population in London from 1970s to 1980s
2. After 1980s, there is a significant trend of population growing in London
3. The growth speed in East London and Boroughs around River Thames are more rapid than others

The changes in population reveal the changes in the economic structure of London. Especially from 1970 to 1980, the closure of London's docks led to massive unemployment, causing jobless people to leave the city. Since 1981, the number of jobs in financial services and knowledge-based industries had increased. Moreover, the redevelopment of London’s docklands in the 1990s, and the more recent redevelopment of the Olympic site in East London, increased the number and variety of jobs available in London, making it a desirable place to work and live. As a result, the population of both inner and outer London has risen ever since 1980. London Docklands is the name for an area in east and southeast London.

The animation of population changes in 33 London boroughs clearly demostrates the speed of population growth throughout the time as well as the ranking also indicates which boroughs are more population aggregated. With the red-coloured bars representing the boroughs associated with Docklands, the rapid growth rate in Docklands can be captured.

Building
Ages

The buildings' age and type data were extracted from dwelling-build-period-and-property-type-2014-lsoa, provided by Valuation Office Agency.
1.Majority of the buildings in outter London were built during the Interwar Expansion period
2.Majority of the dewellings are Flat or Maisonette
3.Buildings in Docklands were mainly built in 21st century

The data in LSOA scale provides more area-specific information about dwellings' age and type. With the choropleth presented, the distribution of London building can be obtained and fascinating stories about London history can be told. For example, the outer layer coloured by orange and light yellow suggests that those buildings were built during “Inter war Expansion” period(1919-1939), which is the period people seeking a more “rural” lifestyle so they left the centre and moved to rural suburbs for a cleaner and greener environment. Meanwhile, the “Metropolitan Green Belt” was introduced to restrict urban growth. Conversely, the blue and white coloured area around River Thames indicates that London Governor has concentrated the resource and focused on development around the center of London since 1980s. Thus, making docklands become our main study object.

Understanding land use change of the Docklands.

Comparing pre 1980s land use with present day.

While it is beneficial to understand the population growth of an area, as McGranahan and Marcotullio (2005) highlight, this alone conceals insight into the dynamics of urban land use change, especially as urban growth often does not determine productivity, liveability, or ‘attractiveness’ (WBG, 2015). Therefore, it is important to look at the disaggregated land use of a specific region, to understand the level of diversity, especially as urban areas often experience highly mixed-land use within a small area.

Land Use
Change

The up-to-date land use data from OpenStreetMap (2021) is available on the third-party sites like Geofabrik (2021), who allow downloads of frequently updated Shapefiles. However, no such data exists for historical land use.

Observations from the 1980s:

  • In the 1980s land use was sparse and prodominately industrial and residential.
  • Residential areas in the 1980s were in close proximtiy to industry sites for dock workers.

    • Present day observations :

  • In present day, almost all of the land has a purpose, with very few vacant areas.
  • The majority of the land has been transformed into residential spaces, whilst some remain terraced housing from the 1990s, investments into new sky rise apartments are also present.
